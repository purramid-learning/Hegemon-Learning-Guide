Math · The Coordinate Plane

Finding points on the coordinate plane

Two numbers are enough to pin down any spot on a grid — once you know which number does which job.

What you'll be able to do

  • Name the parts of the coordinate plane: axes, origin, and quadrants.
  • Write and read an ordered pair in the form (x, y).
  • Plot and locate points in all four quadrants, including negatives.
step 1A line you already know

Start with one number line

You've used a number line. It stretches out from 0 in two directions: positive numbers to the right, negative numbers to the left. Every number marks one exact spot.

One number line is great for one piece of information — like temperature, or how many steps forward you took. But it can't tell you where something is on a flat surface, like a seat in a movie theater or a square on a map. For that, you need a second direction.

step 2Two lines, one plane

Cross two number lines

Take a horizontal number line and a vertical number line, and cross them so their zeros line up. Now you have a coordinate plane — a flat grid where every point has an address.

The two lines are called axes. The horizontal one is the x-axis. The vertical one is the y-axis. The spot where they cross — where both lines read zero — is the origin, written (0, 0).

Notice: the origin isn't "the middle" of the picture — it's the one point where the two axes cross. That's true no matter how much of the grid you can see.
step 3Four regions

The axes make four quadrants

The two axes split the plane into four corners called quadrants. They're numbered with Roman numerals I, II, III, IV, starting at the top-right and going counter-clockwise.

Which quadrant a point lands in depends only on the signs of its two numbers — whether each is positive or negative.

step 4The address of a point

Ordered pairs: (x, y)

A point's address is an ordered pair of two numbers, called its coordinates, written inside parentheses and separated by a comma.

The word ordered is the whole game: the order tells you which number does which job.

  • The first number is the x-coordinate — how far to move across (right if positive, left if negative).
  • The second number is the y-coordinate — how far to move up or down (up if positive, down if negative).

To plot a point, start at the origin, move across first, then up or down. Here's (3, 2): go right 3, then up 2.

Order matters: (3, 2) and (2, 3) are two different points. Swapping the numbers sends you somewhere else entirely.
step 5Going negative

Plotting in every quadrant

The rule never changes when numbers go negative — you just move the other way. A negative x means go left; a negative y means go down.

Look at (−4, 3): from the origin, go left 4, then up 3. That lands in Quadrant II.

Reading a point works in reverse. Find how far it sits across from the origin, then how far up or down. Write the across number first.

And watch for zeros: a point like (0, 4) has no across-movement, so it sits right on the y-axis. A point like (−3, 0) sits on the x-axis.

recapWords to keep

Vocabulary

Coordinate plane
A flat grid formed by two number lines crossing at right angles.
Axes
The two number lines: the x-axis (horizontal) and the y-axis (vertical).
Origin
The point where the axes cross, with coordinates (0, 0).
Ordered pair
Two numbers (x, y) that give a point's address. Order matters.
Coordinates
The two numbers in an ordered pair: the x-coordinate (across) and the y-coordinate (up/down).
Quadrant
One of the four regions (I, II, III, IV) the axes divide the plane into.
